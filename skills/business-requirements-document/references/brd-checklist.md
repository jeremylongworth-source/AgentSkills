# BRD Checklist

## Intake

- Business problem and opportunity are stated.
- Sponsor, decision owner, stakeholders, and affected teams are identified.
- Source material is separated from assumptions and open questions.
- Time horizon, deadline, budget sensitivity, and approval path are clear.

## Scope

- In-scope and out-of-scope items are explicit.
- Current-state and future-state workflows are described when process changes.
- Customer, employee, vendor, operational, and system impacts are considered.
- Constraints cover budget, timing, staffing, policy, compliance, vendor, data,
  technology, and support needs where relevant.

## Requirements Quality

- Requirements are business-facing and avoid premature implementation detail.
- Each requirement is testable or reviewable.
- Priorities use a clear scheme such as must, should, could, will not.
- Business rules are separated from feature ideas and technical design.
- Nonfunctional needs are included when business-critical: availability,
  performance, accessibility, privacy, security, auditability, supportability,
  reporting, and retention.

## Traceability

- Each requirement maps to a business objective.
- Each requirement has an owner or approving stakeholder.
- Acceptance criteria or success validation is listed for each major
  requirement.
- Dependencies, risks, and unresolved decisions are visible.

## Review

- Conflicts and tradeoffs are called out.
- Approval gates are clear before implementation, procurement, or launch.
- Open questions are assigned to owners when possible.
- The BRD can be handed to product, engineering, operations, vendor, finance, or
  leadership without relying on hidden context.
